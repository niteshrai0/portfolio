B2B Embedded Insurance · API Platform · Integration Architecture

Turning Integration Chaos Into a Scalable System

An API-first platform connecting lending institutions with insurance providers — where the core problem wasn't the product, it was the infrastructure holding it together.

The Problem
Every insurance provider spoke a different technical language. Every partner integration was a custom project built from scratch. No standard. No repeatability. What looked like a platform from the outside was a collection of one-off builds held together by institutional memory.
The Thinking
The instinct was to fix integrations one by one. Instead, treated it as an architecture problem with three distinct layers — how data enters the system, how it's processed through a white-label API, and how each provider connects through a standardised framework. Built the system, not the fix.
What Was Built
Three-layer integration infrastructure standardising 60+ unique provider APIs into one repeatable framework. AIRe — an AI-powered recommendation engine. Fully simulated demo environment. Confluence knowledge ecosystem. MENA and Africa market expansion research translated into roadmap priorities.
The Outcome
Platform scaled to 10+ lending partners, 25+ insurers, 120+ live integrations. Partner onboarding became a configuration exercise, not a development project. Support queries fell 25%. The platform reached profitability and closed funding rounds.

What This Taught Me
The hardest part of platform work is rarely the feature everyone can see. It's the layer underneath that decides whether the next ten features are cheap or expensive to build. The right question isn't "what should we build next" — it's "what would make everything we build next easier."
B2B2C Retail Insurance · Multi-Channel Distribution · Zero-to-One

Building a Distribution Platform Across Every Channel and Category

A full-stack retail insurance distribution platform — where the challenge wasn't building one journey, it was building a system that made every subsequent journey faster than the last.

The Problem
A retail distribution platform needed to expand across multiple insurance categories and channels simultaneously — Health, Life, Motor, Commercial Vehicles, across DIY, Assisted, and POSP — without fragmenting into disconnected products. Each category had different regulatory requirements, different customer journeys, and different insurer APIs.
The Thinking
The discipline wasn't launching more — it was launching in a way where the fifth category didn't cost five times as much as the first. Every capability built as a reusable system. White-label, configurable per partner. Each new category treated as a zero-to-one problem, not a template exercise.
What Was Built
Term Life and Endowment E2E journeys — goal-based selling, risk profiling, suitability assessment, multi-insurer integrations. Commercial Vehicle insurance from scratch across a Rs.22,000Cr market. Product comparison framework, inspection orchestration, POSP commission ecosystem, DIY renewal journeys.
The Outcome
3+ new monetisable product categories launched. 10% YoY revenue growth contribution. Distribution reach expanded across DIY, Assisted, and agent channels. All within a focused 8-9 month engagement — each capability built as a reusable platform asset.

Breadth without structure becomes chaos. The real product decision isn't which category to launch — it's how to build the first one in a way that makes the second, third, and fourth ones structurally cheaper. That's the difference between a feature roadmap and a platform strategy.

How I Think

01
Systems over instances
Every feature request is a symptom. The real question is what system, if built correctly, would make this and the next ten requests unnecessary. Build the system, not the fix.
02
Understand the ecosystem before touching the product
The product exists inside a business, which exists inside a market, which exists inside a regulatory and competitive environment. Missing any layer means building the wrong thing with confidence.
03
The product is the GTM
In B2B and B2B2C, the best sales asset is a product that sells itself. Demo environments, onboarding flows, documentation — these aren't support functions. They're the go-to-market strategy.
04
Data ends opinions
Strong opinions are the starting point, not the conclusion. The job is to design the question that data can actually answer — and then be willing to be wrong about the answer.
05
Ownership means the layer beneath the feature
Anyone can ship a feature. Ownership means understanding what happens after it ships — the support queries, the edge cases, the partner confusion, the metric that didn't move. That's where the real product work is.
06
Pattern recognition over precedent
Every business problem feels unique until you've seen the underlying pattern before. The most valuable thing experience builds isn't knowledge of solutions — it's the ability to recognise which problem you're actually solving.
